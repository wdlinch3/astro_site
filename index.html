Laboratory for Astronomy & Astrophysics Research

TJHSST's senior research laboratory for authentic work in astronomy, astrophysics, astronomical data analysis, instrumentation, and computational modeling.

Explore the lab Advanced Astronomy at TJ Astronomy Club

From Learning Astronomy to Doing Astronomy

The lab helps students move beyond isolated exercises into year-long research projects built around precise scientific questions, quantitative evidence, and sustained collaboration.

Students read technical literature, frame research plans, choose appropriate methods, analyze data carefully, and communicate their conclusions in scientific forms.

  • Precise scientific questions
  • Technical literature and research plans
  • Evidence, analysis, and uncertainty
  • Scientific communication and collaboration
  • Quantitative work sustained across the year

Research Modes

Student projects may begin with sky observations, public astronomical archives, numerical models, or the technical systems that make measurement possible.

Observational Astronomy

Planning observations, measuring astronomical sources, and turning sky images into calibrated evidence.

Archival Data

Using public survey catalogs, image archives, and mission data to ask questions that exceed a single observing run.

Computational Astrophysics

Building models and simulations that connect physical assumptions with observable astronomical behavior.

Instrumentation and Methods

Developing careful procedures for measurement, calibration, automation, and data quality.

How Students Work

Lab work is organized around the habits of research: preparation, iteration, critique, documentation, and clear explanation.

Research Development

  • Literature review and journal-club discussion
  • Research proposals and project design
  • Selection of data, tools, and methods

Analysis and Iteration

  • Python and Jupyter-based analysis
  • Modeling, visualization, and uncertainty estimates
  • Regular group meetings and progress reports

Scientific Communication

  • Written reports and research-style papers
  • Conference-style posters
  • Oral presentations and public explanation

Tools and Methods

Students combine computational skill, astronomical data literacy, and physical reasoning to make their projects defensible.

Computation

Python, Jupyter notebooks, numerical modeling, reproducible workflows, visualization, and clear documentation.

Astronomical Data

Images, catalogs, spectra, time series, public archives, and the calibration choices that shape interpretation.

Physical Reasoning

Mechanics, radiation, gravity, statistics, and order-of-magnitude estimates used to connect models with observations.
